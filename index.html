Appendix S1: Extended hypotheses
47
We hypothesized that different crop types in our region are able to provide various resources
48
(e.g. food, nesting/breeding and hiding habitats) required by the beneficial insect taxa. Therefore,
49
both compositional heterogeneity (Shannon–Wiener crop diversity) and configurational
50
heterogeneity (field margin length) may have a positive effect on multiple beneficial insect
51
communities as a whole.
52
Previous studies showed that species with sensitive traits (e.g. larger body, higher-
53
dispersal) cannot adjust to highly intensified systems (de Palma et al., 2015; Purvis, Gittleman,
54
Cowlishaw, & Mace, 2000). Therefore, in highly intensified systems, only generalist species
55
with adapted traits will maintain and trait diversity will be low. By contrast, we hypothesized
56
that different crop types provide diverse resources for both specialists and generalists. Ahigher
57
crop diversity (i.e. compositional crop heterogeneity) will therefore benefit species with different
58
traits and support higher trait diversity in insect communities. Therefore, compositional
59
heterogeneity will have a positive effect on trait diversity across multiple beneficial insect
60
communities.
61
A higher configurational heterogeneity corresponds to landscapes with a high quantity of
62
semi-natural/weedy field margins, that have often low levels of management. In addition, it
63
corresponds to landscapes where habitats (i.e. crop and non-crop) are well-connected (Fahrig et
64
al., 2011). Therefore, a higher configurational heterogeneity can facilitate both specialist and
65
generalist traits. As a result, we expected positive effects of configurational heterogeneity on trait
66
diversity across multiple insect taxa.
67
We further hypothesized that the trait diversity of multiple insect taxa in our region varies
68
between the different crop habitats and in non-crop weedy vegetation. In addition, we expected
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 5
69
trait diversity differences between the different crop types (e.g. Rice, sugarcane/corn, vegetable,
70
see section 2.4. in the main text). We also expected a higher trait diversity in weedy vegetation
71
than in crop habitats, due to their greater habitat quality.

Appendix S2: Field margins
74
Field margins are the boundaries of both crop and non-crop fields, but not the internal boundaries
75
within the crop fields. Farmers use these field boundaries day-to-day, to access their crop fields,
76
and they were composed with non-crop weedy vegetation without any crops on them. The non-
77
crop vegetation on field margins was typically composed with native and non-native grasses,
78
such as Digitaria sanguinalis (Poaceae), Echinochloa crusgalli (Poaceae) and Eleusine indica
79
(Poaceae), and herbs, such as Alternanthera philoxeroides (Amaranthaceae), Lindernia crustacea
80
(Linderniaceae ) and Ludwigia hyssopifolia (Onagraceae) (see Zhou, Lee, & Goodale, 2018).

Appendix S3: Insect sampling protocol and species identification
83
Dung beetle sampling
84
Following Larsen (2005), we set up three pitfall traps (10-cm diameter plastic cups with a bait)
85
along three 50-m long transects (i.e. along field margins), at two ends and in the middle. The
86
traps were left in place for two days. The bait consisted of 30 g of human feces per trap from a
87
person with a fish diet continuously through the study period and wrapped in a 2-mm mesh. The
88
plastic cups were buried along the transects and filled with ca. 40 ml NaCl solution with a few
89
drops of dish soap as a surfactant.
90
Bees
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 6
91
Following Fahrig et al. (2015), we set up three pan traps painted in three different colors (yellow,
92
blue, and white) along each 50-m long transect (i.e. along field margins). We used 45-cm height
93
plastic poles to attach the pans, which allowed us to placed pans abovethe surrounding
94
vegetation, except in sugarcane and corn. The pan was 2/3 filled with water, and a few dish soap
95
drops were added to break surface tension. The pan traps were located at each end of the transect
96
and in the middle. These traps were left in place for two days.
97
Dragonflies/Damselflies and Butterflies
98
We adapted the survey methodology from Fahrig et al. (2015). We walked along the 50-m long
99
transects (i.e. along field margins) at a rate of 2 m per minute (without accounting for the time
100
catching and identifying insects). The sampling was performed when the weather was favorable
101
(i.e.sunny, no rain and no strong wind). All morphospecies encountered during the survey were
102
collected using a sweep net within an imaginary 5-m 3 box. Since the sampling timecan have an
103
influence on the abundance or the activity of these groups (Janzen, 1973), each site was visited
104
twice at two different times of the day (morning vs. afternoon), and the order of the first visit was
105
randomized.
106
Insect Identification
107
We first selected one individual of each morphospecies, and pinned them. The remaining
108
sampleswere preserved in 100% ethyl alcohol. We then made macrophotographs of the pinned-
109
morphospecies from several angles with visible defining traits and sent to regional experts for
110
identification, along with the body-size measurements. We further posted photos on Facebook
111
specialists’ groups and iNaturalist.org, allowing other experts to comment on species identity.
112
We were able to identify > 90% of dung beetle, dragonfly/damselfly and butterfly samples to the

113
species level with only a few to genus level. For bees, we identified > 40% of specimens to
114
species level and the rest to genus or subgenus level.

Appendix S4: Seasonal dynamics of crop types
117
There were 21 and 24 crop types observed respectively in summer and in spring, totaling 26 crop
118
types across both seasons (Fig. S4). Rice (83%) and sugarcane (75%) dominated across
119
farmlands in summer. Rice planting generally happens at the end of the spring in our region,
120
hence only 15% of sites corresponded to rice-fields during our spring fieldwork, whereas 70% of
121
farmlands were composed of sugarcane at that season. As for other crops, corn, peanut, chili and
122
bean were fairly common crops (> 50% of the sites in some seasons), followed by other
123
vegetables such as watermelon, luffa, pumpkin, bottle gourd, bitter gourd, and lettuce (> 35% of
124
the sites in some seasons). In spring, harvested sugarcane-fields were replaced with watermelon,
125
and newly added sugarcane fields were planted on formerly harvested corn fields.

Appendix S5: Trait measurement protocol and ecological relevance of the measured traits
128
Measurements were taken from all the species. If possible, different morphs including both
129
males and females were measured. One to ten specimens of dung beetle and one to three
130
specimens of bee, dragonfly/damselfly and butterfly per species collected at each site (depending
131
on availability) were measured with aid of Leica M205 A stereomicroscope, and Leica
132
Application Suite software (version 4.9.9). The proboscis length of butterflies was measured on
133
live specimens with aid of a Vernier caliper, while all other measurements were taken from dry
134
specimens. The weight of all measured specimens was assessed using a Mettler Toledo XS204
135
analytical balance.
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 8
136
(1) Body length
137
Body length was measured as the total lateral length from anterior of head to the apex of
138
abdomen (in mm). Body length is a measure of body size, and has been shown to be associated
139
with microhabitat conditions and resource-use related traits (e.g. Barton et al. 2011, Dias et al.
140
2017, Grab et al. 2019, Woodcock et al. 2019, Micó et al. 2020, Provosta et al. 2020).
141
(2) Body mass
142
Body mass was measured as the dry weight in mg. Body mass is associated with size-related
143
traits, and it is associated with species metabolism and microhabitat conditions (e.g. Saint-
144
Germain et al. 2007, Woodcock et al. 2010, 2019, Nervo et al. 2014, Dias et al. 2017, Piccini et
145
al. 2018)
146
(3) Eye size
147
Eye size relative to the body length was measured, i.e. eye size = eye length (mm)/body length
148
(mm). Eye size is a sensory trait, which can help a species to visually orientate (Dias et al.,
149
2017). Eye size is also a trait involved in prey and/or predator recognition, and can be associated
150
with the species activity period (e.g. Bauer and Kredler 1993, Raine et al. 2018, Neff et al. 2019,
151
Micó et al. 2020)
152
(4) Wing aspect ratio
153
Wing aspect ratio is a trait related to dispersal ability and disturbance avoidance. The wing
154
aspect ratio was calculated as the wing width (mm)/wing length (mm) (e.g. Berwaerts et al.
155
2002, Raine et al. 2018).
156
(5) Body robustness
157
Body robustness was measured by regressing 10 individual measures on body length (mm) for
158
each taxon and averaging the residuals of these regressions for each species. These 10 individual
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 9
159
measures included: forewing length, hindwing length (except for dung beetles), forewing width,
160
hindwing width (except for dung beetles), head length, head width, thorax width, thorax length,
161
abdomen length, abdomen width (except for butterflies). A more robust species will have higher
162
residuals, indicating higher values in these variables than expected for its body length. This trait
163
is independent of body length, and is associated with microhabitat use (see Barton et al. 2011,
164
Micó et al. 2020). For example, species living in confined microhabitats have flattened body
165
shapes, while species living in open microhabitats have generally more rounded shapes bodies
166
(Barton et al., 2011). It is therefore affecting their defenses against predation and environmental
167
factors.
168
( 6) Hindwing-forewing ratio
169
Hindwing-forewing ratio was calculated as the hindwing length (mm)/forewing length (mm).
170
Forewing–hindwing interaction is essential for insects to control their flight (Jantzen & Eisner,
171
2008; Ji & Sun, 2005; Salcedo, Hoffmann, Donoughe, & Mahadevan, 2019), and therefore it is a
172
measure of dispersal ability and disturbance avoidance.
173
(7) Abdomen size
174
Abdomen size is associated with reproductive capacity of dung beetles. Abdomen size was
175
measured as the thorax length (mm)/abdomen length (mm). This trait is also associated with
176
survival rates in modified lands (e.g. Srygley and Chai 1990, Raine et al. 2018).
177
(8) Hind leg size
178
Hind leg size is associated with nesting and dung relocation (dung transporting and burying) for
179
dung beetles. It was measured as the hind leg length (mm)/body length (mm). Species with
180
greater leg sizes have greater digging abilities (e.g. Raine et al. 2018, Ortega-Martínez et al.
181
2020).
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 10
182
(9) Wing-loading
183
Wing-loading is associated with dispersal and flight ability, and facilitate locating the food
184
source, for dung beetles in particular. Wing-loading was measured as the wing area (mm 2 ) /body
185
area (body area = body length in mm × thorax width in mm) (e.g. Raine et al. 2018, Ortega-
186
Martínez et al. 2020).
187
(10) Mandible size
188
Mandible size was measured relative to the head size, i.e. mandible width (mm)/head width
189
(mm). Mandible size is ássociated with hunting efficiency for predator species (see de la Mora et
190
al. 2008). It can also be a predictor of prey types/sizes consumed by dragonfly/damselfly (e.g.
191
Rüppell and Hilfert-Rüppell 2013, Blanke et al. 2017). This trait is related to resource use and
192
consumption (see Dias et al. 2017).
193
(11) Proboscis length
194
Proboscis length of pollinators determines flower visitation and foraging efficiency (see Kunte
195
2007, Bauder et al. 2013, Cariveau et al. 2016, Klumpers et al. 2019, Woodcock et al. 2019). It
196
can also be a predictor of the plant-pollinator network structure by being a measure of host nectar
197
plant specialization and/or generalization, where a species with longer proboscis has greater
198
specialization (see Goulson et al. 2005, Cariveau et al. 2016, Jain et al. 2016, Klumpers et al.
199
2019, Woodcock et al. 2019). Proboscis length was measured relative to the body size, i.e.
200
proboscis length (mm)/body length (mm). For butterflies, proboscis length was measured on live
201
specimens, following Jain et al. (2016) – a needle was inserted in the center point of the coiled
202
proboscis, and the proboscis was straightened. For bees, proboscis length was measured based on
203
the intertegular distance and their family, using “ tonguelength ” function in the pollimetry
204
package (Kendall, Bartomeus, & Sutter, 2018). R-codes are available in Kendall et al. (2019).
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 11
205
(12) Hairiness index
206
Hairiness is a powerful predictor of pollinator effectiveness, and strongly link to pollen uptake
207
and transportation (see Stavert et al. 2016, Woodcock et al. 2019, Roquer-Beni et al. 2020). In
208
bees, it has been shown that body hairs can detect electromagnetic fields emitted by flowers as
209
pollination cues (Sutton, Clarke, Morley, & Robert, 2016). We therefore measured hairiness
210
index for bees following Woodcock et al. (2019). The index for each bee species was measured
211
by giving scores to body parts, i.e. a score of 0: hairy (coarse setae or extremely short hairs); a
212
score of 1: somewhat hairy (short (c. basal tibiae 1 diameter) but dense hairs); a score of 2: very
213
hairy (dense long (>basal tibiae 1 diameter) dense hairs. These scores were given for the body
214
parts that were likely to be in contact with flower stigmas (head, thorax, abdomen underside,
215
femora 1, 2 and 3, tibiae 1,2 and 3 and meta-tarsus 1, 2 and 3). We then added up the score given
216
for each body part (0,1,2) out of a maximum score of 24 (12 body parts x 2) and gave this as a
217
proportion.
218
(13) Nectar load
219
Nectar load is related to diet and foraging requirements for a nectar feeding species (e.g Harano
220
and Nakamura 2016, Henry and Rodet 2018, Rodney and Purdy 2020). It was measured in 
221
based on the body length (mm), using “ nectarload ” function in the pollimetry package (Kendall
222
et al., 2018). R-codes are available in Kendall et al. (2019).
223
(14) Antenna length
224
Antenna length is a sensory trait, which can help a species to apprehend the surrounding
225
environmental conditions (both physical and chemical), associated with tactile and visual
226
orientation (e.g. Bauer and Kredler 1993, Schütz et al. 1999, Neff et al. 2019). We measured the
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)

227
antenna length of butterflies relative to the body size, i.e. antenna length (mm)/body length
228
(mm).

Appendix S7: Disentangling the overall functional diversity across all the insect taxa
237
We decomposed the overall functional diversity index (FD overall , see section 2.6.2 in the main
238
text) into core sets of independent functional traits across all the different taxa. To do so, we
239
performed a principle component analysis (PCA) considering all traits measured. The PCA was
240
run for each taxon separately (see Fig. S13). This allowed us to identify five sets of independent
241
trait indices (see Fig. S14, S15 for Pearson correlations between each index) across multiple
242
beneficial insect taxa:
243
(1) Overall body size functional diversity index (FD size )
244
This was calculated considering body mass and body length of each taxon, see Appendix S5.
245
(2) Overall body robustness functional diversity index (FD robustness )
246
This was measured by regressing 10 individual measures of four body parts (i.e. wing, head,
247
thorax, abdomen) across each taxon on their body length and averaging the residuals of these
248
regressions for each species, see Appendix S5 for more details.
249
(3) Overall resource-acquisition functional diversity index (FD res-acqui )
Supplementary Information Priyadarshana et al. (2021)
Crop heterogeneity effects on multi-trait diversity, SUPPLEMENTAL 13
250
This includes traits related to resource-use, i.e. mandible size for predatory
251
dragonflies/damselflies, proboscis length for pollinators (bees and butterflies), hairiness index
252
and nectar load for bee pollinators, and the hind leg size of decomposer/nutrient recycler dung
253
beetles were included. Note thatall the dung beetles sampled were tunnelers. Therefore, hind leg
254
size may not be a good predictor of dung-removal activities for our dataset. It has also been
255
suggested that body mass is highly corelated to dung removal efficiency for tunneler dung
256
beetles (Nervo et al., 2014). We therefore also included body mass of dung beetles to our FD res-
257
acqui index (see Appendix S5 for more details).
258
(4) Overall tactile and visual orientation functional diversity index (FD tactics )
259
This index included eye size of all the functional groups, and Antenna length for butterflies (see
260
Appendix S5 for more details).
261
(5) Overall mobility functional diversity index (FD mobility )
262
This index included wing aspect ratio of all the functional groups and hindwing-forewing ratio.
263
For dung beetles, wing-loading was included in this index (see Appendix S5 for more details).

Appendix S9: Model averaging procedure
274
This procedure allows us to freely select any combination of explanatory variables in the full
275
model, including the null model (i.e., intercept only model) under a given  cutoff
276
threshold, and average the regression coefficients across the selected models. When only one
277
model was selected in the model-averaging procedure with  < 4, this model was used as
278
the best model. When there were multiple best models, conditional-averaged coefficients were
279
calculated across all selected models.
